Game Theory · Topic 03 · a JJ original

Rock, Paper, Scissors… SCV!

This lesson exists because JJ invented a game at the dinner table: rock, paper, scissors — plus the SCV, StarCraft's hard-working construction unit. Tonight we do what real game studios do: put the invention on the whiteboard, use graph theory to find out why it's unbalanced, prove that no four-move game can ever be fair, and then fix it — twice. Once JJ's way, with coin flips. Once the designer's way, with a fifth unit.

⏱ ~30 min   uses GT·1 + the bridges lesson   🚜 balance your own game

1 · Put the game on a whiteboard

Draw every move as a dot, and an arrow from each winner to each loser — the same dots-and-arrows trick as the Seven Bridges. Classic rock-paper-scissors becomes a perfect three-arrow cycle:

🪨 → ✂️ → 📄 → 🪨

Why does classic RPS feel fair? Count each move's record: everything beats exactly 1 and loses to exactly 1. Score: beats minus loses = 0 for everyone. That's what balanced means — no move is secretly the best, so every choice stays alive. Now let's add the SCV and watch the balance break.

2 · The impossible patch: four moves can NEVER be fair

The SCV needs a relationship with each old move: 🪨–🚜, 📄–🚜, ✂️–🚜. Three new arrows, and each must point one way or the other. Try every combination yourself — flip the arrows and watch the power meter:

⚖️ The balance bench

Click an arrow button to flip who wins. Score = beats − loses. Can you get all four bars to zero?

Flip away. (Spoiler: you can't win this one — and proving WHY is the lesson.)

You can't do it — and here's the beautiful two-line proof. Look at rock: its old score is 0 (beats scissors, loses to paper). Its new SCV arrow either helps it (+1) or hurts it (−1). So rock's score is now +1 or −1 — never 0 again. Same for paper and scissors. Adding a fourth move ruins every old move's perfect record, no matter which way the arrows point.

The counting version: 4 moves make $\frac{4 \times 3}{2} = 6$ pairs (the handshake formula — third lesson in a row it shows up!). Fair means everyone wins the same share: $6 \div 4 = 1.5$ arrows each. But an arrow can't point one-and-a-half ways. Whole arrows split evenly only when the number of moves is odd. That's a real theorem, and it's why the famous five-move game (rock-paper-scissors-lizard-Spock) has five.

3 · JJ's patch: the coin flip (half an arrow!)

At the dinner table JJ proposed something game designers get paid for: if two SCVs meet, flip a coin. A coin-flip matchup is like an arrow that points half a way each — worth $+\tfrac12$ and $-\tfrac12$ at once. Suddenly “1.5 arrows each” is possible! But which matchups should be coin flips? Slide the SCV's win-chances and hunt for perfect balance:

🎚️ The probability workshop

Each slider = the old move's chance of beating the SCV. Score = expected wins minus expected losses. Find the setting where all four bars hit zero — there is exactly ONE. (⭐ when you nail it.)

🪨 beats 🚜 …100%
📄 beats 🚜 …0%
✂️ beats 🚜 …0%
Starting from JJ's dinner-table rules: rock smashes the SCV, but the SCV beats scissors AND paper. Check the meter…

The unique answer: every single SCV matchup must be a 50/50 coin flip. The proof is the same two-liner as before — rock's score is $2w - 1$ where $w$ is its chance against the SCV, and that's zero only when $w = \tfrac12$. Each old move forces its own slider to the middle, and then the SCV balances automatically. JJ's coin-flip instinct wasn't just a fun rule — it's the only way to balance four moves.

Balance is necessary… but it isn't the whole job. Real patch notes wrestle with exactly this trade-off.

4 · The designer's fix: call in the Zergling

The theorem said fair-without-coins needs an odd number of moves. So don't fight the theorem — recruit a fifth unit. And there's a construction that always works: put the five moves in a circle, and let each move beat the next two clockwise. Everyone beats 2, everyone loses to 2. Balanced by design, for any odd number.

Now the genuinely amazing part. Arrange the circle as 🪨 Rock → 🚜 SCV → ✂️ Scissors → 📄 Paper → 👾 Zergling and check the arrows:

⭕ The pentagon of power
🪨 beats 🚜 ✂️🚜 beats ✂️ 📄✂️ beats 📄 👾📄 beats 👾 🪨👾 beats 🪨 🚜
Every rule JJ made up at dinner survived: 🪨 smashes the 🚜 (through the window!), the 🚜 welds ✂️ shut and builds right over 📄's blueprint. And the two new arrows are pure StarCraft: 👾 zerglings rush 🪨 defenses and — every Terran's nightmare — eat worker 🚜s.

All three classic rules: intact. All three of JJ's SCV rules: intact. Zero coin flips. Every move beats exactly two and loses to exactly two — the dinner-table game, balanced without changing a single rule JJ wrote. (Only the two brand-new 👾 arrows needed choosing, and StarCraft chose them for us.) This is the actual workflow of balance designers: the flavor comes from the inventor, the structure comes from the theorem.

📓 Homework

  1. Flavor the weird arrows. The theorem picked two arrows nobody asked for: ✂️ beats 👾 and 📄 beats 👾. Invent the in-universe reasons (why WOULD paper stop a zergling? Supply block? Wrapped like a burrito?). A balance patch isn't shipped until the flavor text is written.
  2. Playtest it. Teach a friend the pentagon (draw it — each move beats the next two) and play 20 rounds. Does any move feel too strong? The math says no — does the fun agree?
  3. Design the 7-move game. The circle trick works for any odd number: 7 moves, each beating the next 3. Pick 7 StarCraft units, draw the circle, and check one unit's score by hand. (How many pairs of moves are there in total? You know the formula.)

The Art of Leaving Out

A camera can't help drawing everything it sees. An artist's superpower is the opposite: not drawing. When a mug stands in front of a book, the book's edge must stop at the mug and pick up again on the other side — or turn into a faint ghost, if you want an x-ray view. Tonight: how a machine decides, for every millimetre of every stroke, draw or don't.

⏱ ~30 min   good after SK·1 + SK·2   🩻 earn x-ray eyes

1 · One question per millimetre

The rule sounds almost too simple. Take any point on any stroke you're about to draw. Run a straight line from your eye to that point. Does the line pass through solid stuff on the way? If yes, that point is hidden. If no, it's visible. That's the whole law — the same eye-ray thinking as the grazing rays of last lesson, but now asking blocked or clear? instead of kissing or missing?

The craft is in doing this to a whole stroke at once. A stroke is a range — think of it as the interval from 0 to 100 — and each blocker in front of it bites out a sub-interval. What's left is the visible part: possibly several separate pieces. The sketch engine from the Studio computes the exact bite points by solving equations (no guessing, no pixel-testing), then draws each surviving piece as its own clean stroke.

Ghost lines are a real tool: engineers and architects draw hidden edges as faint dashes on purpose — an x-ray view tells a builder what's inside the wall. The engine calls the two styles drop (paper-honest) and ghost (x-ray). Same math, different ink.

2 · The piece counter

One subtlety makes this a real puzzle: blockers can overlap. Two pots standing shoulder to shoulder bite one merged bite, not two. So before counting pieces you must merge overlapping bites — then a stroke crossing behind k separate merged bites survives in at most k+1 pieces (fewer if a bite swallows an end of the stroke!).

Try this tonight

  • Human hidden-line remover: line up a fork standing behind a cup. Draw the fork but ONLY the parts you can actually see. Feel how weirdly hard it is not to draw what you know is there — the machine has no such temptation.
  • Count pieces for real: look at power lines behind a tree. One wire, how many visible pieces? Move two steps left — now how many?
  • X-ray archaeology: find a diagram of a pyramid or a submarine with dashed hidden lines. Every dash is a deliberate “ghost” decision by the artist.

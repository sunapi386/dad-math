Circuits: The Adding Machine

You met logic gates on the Factorio page and built a half adder there. Tonight you learn the notation real engineers live by — the truth table, a gate's ID card — then use it like a detective to unmask a mystery gate, meet the one gate that can build all the others, and chain full adders into a working 4-bit adding machine: the actual circuit doing the arithmetic inside every chip you own.

⏱ ~30 min   best after CS·1 + the Factorio page   🔌 operate a real adder

1 · The truth table: a gate's ID card

A gate is a tiny machine with a personality: give it inputs, it always answers the same way. A truth table writes that whole personality down — one row per possible input combo, nothing left out. With 2 inputs there are exactly 2\times2 = 4 combos, so 4 rows. Three inputs? 8 rows. It's the binary doubling again: n wires → 2n rows.

Why bother with a table when the sentence “on if both are on” works? Because sentences get fuzzy and tables can't. Two circuits with the same truth table are the same circuit, no matter how differently they're wired inside — that one idea lets engineers swap billions of parts without fear.

2 · Two upside-down gates — and a king

Take AND's table and flip every answer: you get NAND (“not-and”). Flip OR and you get NOR. They look like afterthoughts. They are royalty:

NAND is universal: feed a wire into both of a NAND's inputs and it acts as NOT (check row 1 and row 4 above). Put that NOT after a NAND and you've rebuilt AND. A few more and you have OR, XOR — everything. A whole computer can be one gate, copied billions of times. This isn't a party trick: the Apollo Guidance Computer that flew people to the Moon was built almost entirely from one little NOR chip, about 5,600 copies of it.

3 · The mystery box

Now play engineer-detective. A chip arrives with the label scratched off — inside is one of the five gates you know: AND, OR, XOR, NAND, NOR. You can't open it. You can only probe it: set the inputs, read the output. This is twenty questions all over again — every probe splits the suspects, and a good probe splits them hard.

🕵️ Unmask the gate

Each probe works once. When you're sure, click the suspect to accuse. Three unmaskings in a row = the 🔌 Circuit Detective badge. (Three clever probes always crack it — can you find a case where two are enough?)

Detective's secret: the probe 1·0 never tells you anything the probe 0·1 didn't — all five suspects treat their two inputs the same way (they're symmetric). So you really have 3 useful probes for 5 suspects… and it still always works. Count how each probe splits the survivors, like the monster game.

4 · One column of addition: the full adder

On the Factorio page you built the half adder: sum = a XOR b, carry = a AND b. It adds the last column of a binary sum. But look at any middle column of addition — it receives three bits: a, b, and the carry coming in from the column before. The circuit for that is the full adder, and its whole personality is a 3-input table with 2^3 = 8 rows:

5 · Chain them: the 4-bit adding machine

Now the payoff. Put four full adders in a row. Each column's carry out feeds the next column's carry in — a bucket brigade for bits. Engineers call it a ripple-carry adder, because a carry can ripple down the whole chain like dominoes. This exact circuit (stretched to 64 columns) is inside your computer doing billions of additions every second.

The machine is live — flip some bits.
Why engineers lose sleep: in the 15 + 1 ripple, the answer isn't right until the carry has marched through every adder, one at a time. On a 64-bit chip that march is the slow part — so real chips use clever “carry-lookahead” circuits that predict the carries ahead of time. Same truth table, faster wiring. Remember: same table = same circuit.

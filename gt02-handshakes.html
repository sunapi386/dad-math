Six Handshakes to Anyone

Pick anyone alive — a movie star, a fisherman in Norway, the kid who'll be your best friend in ten years. The claim: a chain of about six friendships connects you to them. Eight billion people, six steps. Tonight we find out why that's not magic — it's multiplication — and why it almost fails, except for a trick you can build with five clicks.

⏱ ~30 min   best after G·1: Seven Bridges   🌍 shrink a world yourself

1 · The most powerful kind of counting

In graph language (dots = people, lines = friendships): how many people can you reach in $d$ hops? Suppose everyone knows about $k$ people. You know $k$. Their friends: $k \times k$. Friends-of-friends-of-friends: $k \times k \times k$. Each handshake multiplies:

$$\text{reach in } d \text{ handshakes} \approx k^d$$

Adding grows like a snail; multiplying grows like an avalanche (you saw this in CS·1 — every extra bit doubles the numbers). If everyone knows just 44 people, then $44^6 \approx 7{,}300{,}000{,}000$ — six handshakes swallow the planet.

2 · The catch: your friends know each other

The $k^d$ avalanche has a cheat in it. It assumes every handshake reaches $k$ new people. Real life isn't like that — your friends mostly know your other friends. Your class, your street, your team: friendship comes in clumps. A handshake inside the clump reaches almost nobody new.

Here's a whole world built only of clumps: 48 people around a ring, each knowing just their 4 nearest neighbours. No avalanche — news crawls around the circle like a bucket brigade. The average distance between two people here is 6.4 hops, and opposite sides are 12 apart. Scale that ring up to Earth and “six handshakes” dies — you'd need millions.

So why is the real world small? In 1998, two mathematicians (Watts and Strogatz) found the answer, and it's beautiful: clumpy worlds need only a few random shortcuts — the aunt in Australia, the pen-pal, the kid who moved schools — and distances collapse for everyone, even people nowhere near a shortcut. Did you find the airport trick? When we let a computer search for the best 5 bridges, it didn't spread them evenly — it kept attaching them to one busy node, inventing a hub. Real networks do this too: airlines, the internet, and that one kid who somehow knows everyone. Hubs are how small worlds get even smaller.

3 · Memes and colds ride the same wires

Press 🦠 spread a meme up there and watch it wash over the ring, day by day — each day it jumps one handshake. On the plain ring it takes 12 days to reach the far side. Add your bridges and watch the same meme cross the world in 3 or 4.

Here's the grown-up secret: that animation doesn't care what is spreading. A joke, a song, a rumour — or a cold. Same graph, same speed. This is why one sneeze in an airport can reach every continent in a week (airports are shortcut factories), and why in 2020 the whole world suddenly cared about network math: cancelling flights and gatherings is literally deleting shortcut edges to make the world temporarily big again. You just did the reverse with five clicks.

4 · Was six ever measured?

Yes — with paper. In 1967 Stanley Milgram mailed packets to random people in Nebraska: “Get this to a stockbroker near Boston — but only by mailing it to someone you know personally.” The letters that arrived took a median of six mailings. Facebook re-ran the experiment on its whole friendship graph in 2016: average distance 3.57 — the internet is one giant shortcut machine, so the world got smaller. The next time someone says “small world!” at a party, you now know it's a theorem, not a coincidence.

📓 Homework

  1. Map a real clump. Draw your class as a graph: dots for kids, lines for real friendships. Find the clumps. Now find the shortcuts — who's friends across clumps? Those kids are your class's airports.
  2. Chain to a celebrity. Pick someone famous and build the shortest chain of people you can argue actually know each other, starting from you. (Parents count. Parents' coworkers count. You'll be shocked how often 4 is enough.)
  3. Explain the 2020 trick. Using the words shortcut and average distance, explain to a grown-up why cancelling flights slows a virus even though most people never fly.

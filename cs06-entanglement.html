Entanglement: The Spooky Phone

In CS·5 you steered one qubit. Tonight: two qubits that share a single fate — the connection Einstein refused to believe and called “spooky action at a distance.” You'll run the actual experiment that proved him wrong (it won the 2022 Nobel Prize), catch an impostor with statistics, and find out why the spookiest phone in the universe still can't send a text.

⏱ ~30 min   best after CS·5 + the cat story   📞 run a real Bell test

1 · Two dials, one fate

Remember the glove boxes from the Cat in the Box? Here's the laboratory version. A machine in the middle fires out a pair of qubits, one to your dial, one to your friend's dial across town. Each dial measures its qubit and prints a 0 or a 1 — a perfect coin-flip, every single time, on both ends.

But the two coin-flips are not strangers. Set both dials to the same angle and the answers always match. Twist them apart and they start to disagree — by an amount the universe computes with a formula you already own from CS·5:

$$\text{chance the answers match} = \cos^2(\text{angle between the dials})$$

📞 The spooky phone

Set each dial (15° steps), then ring the phones — the machine sends one entangled pair and both dials answer at the same instant. Same angle → always a match. Twisted 90° apart → never. Try 45°.

The spooky part: neither qubit has an answer before you ring. Each side alone is a perfect coin-flip (check the tally — about half your answers are 1 no matter what). Yet the two flips agree. It's not that both coins were secretly loaded the same way at the factory… or is it? A good scientist checks. That's section 2.

2 · The impostor: a glove factory

Einstein's explanation was simple and sensible: the answers were decided at the factory. Like a pair of gloves packed in boxes — open one, find LEFT, and of course the other is RIGHT. No spookiness, just a secret decided in advance that you only discovered later.

So imagine an impostor company, Gloves Inc., that sells fake spooky phones. Inside each pair it hides a little answer sheet — the same sheet in both phones — that pre-decides the answer for every dial angle. Same dial, same sheet, same answer: it matches perfectly at equal angles, just like the real thing. At twisted angles their sheets disagree sometimes too. From the outside the two machines look almost identical.

Almost. For thirty years everyone believed no experiment could ever tell “decided at the factory” from “decided at the moment of the call.” Then in 1964 the physicist John Bell found a crack: a score that answer sheets cannot beat — and the real universe does.

Here's Bell's trick, kid-sized. Use just three dial settings: 0°, 30°, 60°. Any answer sheet writes down one answer for each — say 1, 1, 0. Now count the disagreements between pairs of settings:

Stare at that table (those are half the sheets; the rest are mirror images). If a sheet's 0° and 60° answers differ, it must have changed its mind somewhere along the way — at the first step or the second. An answer can't change without changing! So for answer sheets, every single time:

$$\text{disagreement}(0°\!,60°) \;\le\; \text{disagreement}(0°\!,30°) + \text{disagreement}(30°\!,60°)$$

That's just counting — it holds for one sheet, so it holds for millions of sheets averaged together, no matter how cleverly the factory mixes them. Gloves Inc. cannot break it.

3 · The impossible score

Now ask the real spooky phone. Its disagreement rate is $\sin^2$ of the twist (that's the flip side of the $\cos^2$ match rule). Read the three settings off the formula:

0° vs 30°: sin²(30°) = 25%  ·  30° vs 60°: sin²(30°) = 25%  ·  0° vs 60°: sin²(60°) = 75%

But the sheet rule says the big twist can disagree at most 25% + 25% = 50%. The universe answers 75%. That's not a small miss — it's a score no answer sheet, no secret plan, no “decided at the factory” story can ever produce. Run it yourself:

Real laboratories ran exactly this (with photons, mirrors and very fast switches) — Clauser in the 70s, Aspect in the 80s, Zeilinger's group across whole cities. The universe broke the sheet limit every time. In 2022 the three of them shared the Nobel Prize in Physics for it. Einstein's sensible explanation is dead: the answers truly do not exist until the call — and yet the two ends still agree. That is entanglement.

The fake is the best sheet-mixer money can buy — it matches perfectly at equal angles and gets suspiciously close at twisted ones (33% · 33% · 67% — right at the sheet limit). The real thing scores 25% · 25% · 75%. The bars wobble because samples are small — read carefully, then accuse.

5 · The phone that can't send a text

“Wait,” says every kid (and half the physicists of 1935). “The dials agree instantly, even across a galaxy. Faster than light! Let's send messages!” Try it. You're on the left dial; your friend far away wiggles theirs — or unplugs it entirely. If their choice changed anything you can see on your side, that's a message.

Your side is a 50/50 coin no matter what they do. The agreement only appears when you two meet later and compare notes — and the notes travel by ordinary mail, slower than light. So relativity survives, Einstein keeps that victory, and the “spooky phone” is really a spooky coincidence generator: useless for texting, but priceless for quantum computers (entangled qubits are how CS·5's machines link their arrows into one big lean) and for unbreakable secret keys — because if a spy taps the line, the impossible score drops to a possible one, and the Bell test from section 3 catches them red-handed.

📓 Homework

  1. Explain the crack. Using the answer-sheet table, show a grown-up why a sheet whose 0° and 60° answers differ MUST also differ at one of the small steps. Then tell them the universe's three numbers (25, 25, 75) and let them be bothered by it.
  2. Be Gloves Inc. Write secret answer sheets for 10 pairs of paper “phones” (one answer for each of 0°, 30°, 60° — same sheet in both phones of a pair). Have someone test them at the three settings and tally disagreements. Check: does the big-twist row stay under the two small rows added together? (It has to. You're a sheet.)
  3. The no-texting rule. Your friend claims wiggling their dial sends you a message faster than light. Explain what your own tape looks like no matter what they do — and what extra, slower-than-light step is needed before the spooky agreement is even visible.

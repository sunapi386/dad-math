How to Sort a Messy Bookshelf

Every leaderboard, every inventory screen, every photo app quietly does the same chore billions of times a day: putting things in order. Today you'll invent a sorting machine, discover it hides a formula you already derived, find the one trick that makes it thousands of times faster — and learn what sorted order buys you: mind-reading.

⏱ ~30 min   uses Lesson 3's handshake trick   📚 beat par, climb levels

1 · The robot librarian's rules

You're a robot with a messy shelf. You can't “just see” where everything goes — a computer never can. You get exactly two moves, repeated as many times as you like:

🔍 Compare two books — which one comes first?
🔄 Swap two books that are next to each other.

That's it. Every sorting method ever invented — and your phone runs one every time you open it — is just a plan for which compares and swaps to do in which order. A precise plan like that is called an algorithm. Let's invent the most obvious one.

2 · The obvious plan: bubble sort

Walk down the shelf. Whenever two neighbours are out of order, swap them. Reach the end? Walk it again. Stop when a whole walk needs no swaps. Watch it go — big books “bubble” to the end of the shelf, one walk at a time:

3 · What does it cost? (You already know this formula)

Count the work in the worst case. The first walk down a shelf of $n$ books makes $n-1$ compares. And you might need a walk for every book. Add it up…

$$(n-1) + (n-2) + \dots + 2 + 1 = \frac{n(n-1)}{2}$$

Wait. That's the handshake formula from Lesson 3! Bubble sort's worst case is every book “shaking hands” with every other book. And that formula has a nasty habit: double the books, quadruple the work.

10 books → 45 compares. Fine.  100 books → 4,950. Hmm.  A million books (one music app's song list) → about 500 billion compares. Even at a billion compares per second, that's over 8 minutes of pure sorting. Your phone would feel broken. Hold that thought — first, let's see how good you are with swaps.

🏁 GAME · Sort Sprint

Tap a tile to swap it with its right-hand neighbour. Sort the shelf in the fewest possible swaps — hit par exactly and you level up to a bigger shelf (your level is saved). First perfect sort earns the 📚 Shelf Master badge.

swaps 0par ?level 1
Green tiles are home. Get them all green.
Secret of par: count the pairs of tiles that are out of order (a big number sitting anywhere before a smaller one). Each neighbour-swap can fix exactly one such pair, no more — so that count is the true minimum. Computer scientists call those pairs inversions. Now you know why wild shuffles have a bigger par.

4 · The trick that makes it fast: split, sort, merge

Here's the insight that changed everything. Suppose your shelf is somehow already in two sorted halves. Gluing them into one sorted shelf is easy: look at the two front books, take the smaller, repeat. One compare per book you place. No walking back and forth.

So: split the shelf in half. Split the halves in half. Keep going until every “pile” is a single book — a single book is already sorted! Then merge your way back up. That's merge sort, and its plan is called divide and conquer.

The cost: each level of merging touches all $n$ books once, and halving $n$ down to 1 takes $\log_2 n$ levels (that's the “how many times can I cut it in half” number — for a million it's just 20, because $2^{20}$ is about a million). Total:

$$n \log_2 n \quad\text{instead of}\quad \frac{n(n-1)}{2}$$

A million books: ~20 million compares instead of ~500 billion — about 25,000× faster. Same shelf, same rules, better plan. That's what “algorithm” really means: the plan is the speed.

5 · What sorted order buys you: mind-reading

Why do phones bother keeping everything sorted? Because finding things in sorted order is absurdly fast. Guess a number from 1 to 1000: each guess, you're told higher or lower — so guess the middle and half the possibilities vanish. 1000 → 500 → 250 → 125 → … → 1. Ten guesses, guaranteed, every time. (Remember the CS·1 mind reader? Same magic: each answer is one bit.) This halving game is called binary search, and it's the payoff for every sort your phone ever ran.

📓 Homework

  1. Family Sort Sprint. Deal ten playing cards face-up in a row. Sorting rule: only neighbour-swaps, and every swap counts. Count the out-of-order pairs first to predict par — then challenge a grown-up to beat your swap count.
  2. Be the merge sort. Split half a deck into two piles, sort each pile, then merge them using only look-at-the-two-front-cards. Time it against sorting the whole pile bubble-style. Which felt faster? Which needed fewer looks?
  3. Dictionary mind-reader. Have someone pick any word in a real dictionary. Find its page only by opening to the middle of the remaining pages and asking “before or after?”. Count your cuts — for a 1000-page dictionary, how many should you need at most?

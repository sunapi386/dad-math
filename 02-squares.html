Lesson 02

Odd Numbers Make Squares

Add up the odd numbers — 1, then 1+3, then 1+3+5 — and something magic happens: you always land exactly on a perfect square. Today we'll see why, with dots.

⏱ ~30 min   follows Lesson 1

0 · Warm-up: what's a square number?

Remember the orange diagonal of the multiplication table? 1, 4, 9, 16, 25, \dots Those are square numbers — 1{\times}1,\ 2{\times}2,\ 3{\times}3,\dots We call them “squares” because you can arrange that many dots into an actual square:

$$2\times 2 = 4 \qquad 3\times 3 = 9 \qquad 4 \times 4 = 16$$

1 · Only add the odd numbers

Last time we added every number. Today, only the odd ones: 1, 3, 5, 7, 9, … Add them one at a time and keep a running total:

$$1 = 1 \qquad 1+3 = 4 \qquad 1+3+5 = 9 \qquad 1+3+5+7 = 16$$
Notice anything? 1, 4, 9, 16 … those are exactly the square numbers from the warm-up! Let's make it a hypothesis and watch why it happens.

2 · Build it, one L-shape at a time

Here's the secret. To turn an n\times n square into the next (n{+}1)\times(n{+}1) square, you add an L-shaped border along one side and the bottom. Count the dots in that L — it's always the next odd number.

🟧 Gnomon builder (a gnomon is that L-shape)

Press Add next odd. The new orange L is the odd number you're adding; the whole thing stays a perfect square.

3 · The pattern in symbols

After n odd numbers, the total is exactly n^2:

🎛️ Odd-sum explorer

Slide to choose how many odd numbers to add. The sum is always a perfect square.

The n-th odd number is 2n-1 (put n=1 → 1, n=2 → 3, n=3 → 5 …), so what we found is:

$$\boxed{\;1 + 3 + 5 + \cdots + (2n-1) \;=\; n^2\;}$$

4 · Bonus: it also falls out of Lesson 1

Pair the odd numbers Gauss-style — first + last = 1 + (2n-1) = 2n, and there are n/2 pairs:

$$\frac{n}{2}\times 2n = n^2$$

Same answer, straight from last week's trick. Two roads, one destination. □

Lesson 04

Evens & the Parity Trick

Is 347 + 592 odd or even? You can know without adding. Today we learn the parity rules, sum up the even numbers — and finally crack the mystery challenge from Lesson 1.

⏱ ~30 min   solves Lesson 1's challenge

1 · Even means “pairs up perfectly”

A number is even if you can split that many dots into pairs with none left over. It's odd if exactly one dot is left standing alone. That's all parity means: is there a loner, or not?

$$\text{even} = 2n \qquad\qquad \text{odd} = 2n - 1$$

2 · The parity machine

What happens when you add two numbers? Watch the loners.

⚙️ Parity machine

Pick two numbers. Dots pair up in twos; a lonely orange dot means odd. Watch what the loners do when the piles merge.

Two loners find each other and make a pair! That gives us the parity rules — true for any numbers, however huge:

$$\text{even}+\text{even}=\text{even}\qquad \text{even}+\text{odd}=\text{odd}\qquad \text{odd}+\text{odd}=\text{even}$$

Multiplication has rules too: \text{even}\times\text{anything}=\text{even} (pairs stay paired when you copy them), and \text{odd}\times\text{odd}=\text{odd}.

So: 347 + 592? odd + even = odd. No adding required. You just judged a sum you never computed — that's real mathematician behaviour.

3 · Add up the even numbers

Lesson 1 added all numbers; Lesson 2 added the odds. One family left. What is 2+4+6+\cdots+2n? Easy — every term is double a term from Lesson 1, so the whole sum is double the Gauss sum:

$$2+4+6+\cdots+2n \;=\; 2\,(1+2+3+\cdots+n) \;=\; 2\cdot\frac{n(n+1)}{2}$$
$$\boxed{\;2+4+6+\cdots+2n \;=\; n(n+1)\;}$$
🎛️ Even-sum explorer

Slide n. The sum of the first n even numbers is a perfect n × (n+1) rectangle — the same rectangle from Lesson 3, no halving needed.

4 · The payoff: Lesson 1's mystery, solved

Remember the challenge? The Gauss formula \frac{n(n+1)}{2} divides by 2 — yet the answer is always a whole number, even when n is odd. Now you have the tool:

The argument: n and n+1 are neighbours — consecutive numbers. Neighbours always alternate odd, even, odd, even… so one of them must be even. And even × anything = even. So n(n+1) is always even — and an even number splits by 2 with nothing left over. Whole number, guaranteed. □

That's a real proof — not “it worked for the ones I tried,” but “it can never fail, and here's why.” It covers all infinity of cases in three sentences.

The Range Ring

In Core Keep, thirty times a second, every turret asks about every bug: “can I hit that?” — and the ring around the turret is never wrong. Tonight you'll learn the one line of math that answers it, and the sneaky trick inside that line: the game never takes a square root. Not once. Ever.

⏱ ~30 min   Pythagoras, live   📡 judge in-range by eye

1 · The question the turret asks

A turret sits on its cell. A bug walks the road. “In range” means: the straight-line distance between them is at most the turret's range r. But the game doesn't store distances — it stores positions. From two positions you get two easy numbers: how far apart they are across (dx) and how far up-down (dy). Those two are legs of a right triangle, and the distance you actually want is the slanted side. That's Pythagoras — the same math your Deep Rock hitboxes lesson used to test bullet hits:

$$d^2 = dx^2 + dy^2$$

Notice what we computed: not the distance — the squared distance. Getting d itself would need a square root, and here's the professional secret: you never need it. You only want to compare d \le r, and both sides are positive, so it's the exact same question as d^2 \le r^2. Square the range once, compare squares forever. This is the actual line from the engine running the game you played:

var d2 = dx * dx + dy * dy;
if (d2 <= r2) { /* 🐛 in range — fire! */ }

Square roots are slow and messy (√13 = 3.6055512…); multiplications are instant and exact. Every real game engine on Earth does range checks this way.

2 · Walk the grid

📡 The ring inspector

The turret is fixed. Click any cell to put the bug there — the right triangle appears and the math runs. Drag the range slider and watch which cells the ring swallows.

click a cell…
The ring holds every cell whose d² ≤ r². Count them below.

One more treasure is hiding in that picture. Count the cells inside the ring: with r = 3 you'll find 29. And the circle's area is \pi r^2 = \pi \cdot 9 \approx 28.3. The count of grid points inside a circle runs shockingly close to its area — mathematicians call chasing the exact error the Gauss circle problem (your old friend from Lesson 1), and it's still not fully solved. Your turret's coverage is an open research problem wearing a costume.

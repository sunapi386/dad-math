Where Outlines Live

Hand an artist a ball and they draw… a single curve. Where exactly on the ball is that curve? Tonight's answer is sneaky and beautiful: the outline is where your gaze grazes the surface — and on a ball, that grazing circle is not the equator. It's a smaller circle, closer to you, and one right triangle tells you everything about it.

⏱ ~30 min   uses Pythagoras — 3·4·5 triangles   🌒 find the rim

1 · The grazing gaze

Look at any smooth object. Most of your sight-lines either hit the surface square-on (that's the middle of the shape) or miss it entirely (that's the background). But a knife-thin family of sight-lines does neither: they graze the surface, kissing it at exactly one point and sliding past. Those grazing points are the outline. Artists feel this with their eyes; a sketching machine has to solve for it.

For a ball it's pure Pythagoras. A grazing sight-line is tangent to the ball, and a tangent always meets the radius at a right angle. Eye at distance d, ball of radius r: the triangle eye–centre–graze-point has a right angle at the graze point, so the gaze travels

$$t = \sqrt{d^2 - r^2} \qquad\text{before it kisses the ball.}$$

The surprise: the rim chord in the picture sits in front of the ball's centre — closer to your eye. Walk closer and it slides even closer to you (and gets smaller): you see less than half the ball, always. Only a camera at infinity (orthographic — last lesson!) sees the true equator and a full half. You have literally never seen half a basketball at once.

2 · The rim, measured

Two more facts fall out of the same right triangle (both are similar-triangle one-liners):

  • Rim radius = r \cdot t / d — a little smaller than r.
  • How much skin you see = (1 - r/d)/2 of the ball — from d = 2r you see just 25%, from d = 10r you're up to 45%, and 50% needs d = \infty.

Here's why a machine bothers with the equation instead of feeling it out: the engine in the Studio (it's called Krbn) doesn't march around the ball testing thousands of dots. It solves for the outline as an exact curve — for a ball a circle, for a cylinder or a cone other conics, for a donut a wilder quartic curve — and then draws that one perfect curve. Zoom in forever and it never turns to pixel stairs. That's also why the output can drive a pen plotter: it's genuine geometry, not a grid of dots.

A ball and an eye come up — compute the gaze length t = √(d² − r²). They're all secretly 3·4·5-family triangles, so the answers come out whole. Streak of 3 = the 🌒 Rim Finder badge.

Try this tonight

  • The orange test: hold an orange at arm's length. You now know you're seeing about 40% of its peel. Where's the rim circle? Mark your best guess with a marker, then check: is your mark in front of the “equator” facing you?
  • Moon watch: the Moon is ~110 moon-radii away — so you see (1 − 1/110)/2 ≈ 49.5% of it. Almost, but never, half. (Libration wobbles this a bit — look it up together.)
  • Outline hunt: pick any mug. Trace where its outline lives with a finger. Notice the outline of the rim opening is a different curve than the silhouette of the body — a sketch engine finds each one separately.

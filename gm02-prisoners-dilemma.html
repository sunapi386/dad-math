The Prisoner's Dilemma

The most famous game in all of game theory. Two players, two buttons, and a trap: the move that is always smarter for you makes everyone poorer. It explains littering, arms races, and why your team's group project fell apart — and the escape hatch is one simple idea: play again tomorrow.

⏱ ~30 min   plays great after GT·1   🤝 get rich together

1 · The deal

You and a partner each stand at a machine with two buttons, choosing secretly and at the same time:

🤝 SHARE — put a coin in. The machine doubles it and pays it to the other player.
😈 CHEAT — put nothing in, but still grab whatever comes out of your slot.

partner 🤝 sharespartner 😈 cheats
you 🤝 shareyou +3 · them +3you +0 · them +5
you 😈 cheatyou +5 · them +0you +1 · them +1

The whole dilemma is hiding in one line of inequalities: $5 > 3 > 1 > 0$. Cheating a sharer pays best (5), sharing together is good (3), cheating together is bad (1), and being the only sharer — the sucker — pays nothing.

2 · The trap: cheating is always smarter

Here's the poisonous logic. You don't know what your partner will do — but you don't need to:

If they share: cheat pays you 5, share pays you 3. Cheat wins.
If they cheat: cheat pays you 1, share pays you 0. Cheat wins again.

A move that beats every possible reply is called a dominant strategy. Play five rounds against five different strangers and watch the regret meter — the other choice would never have paid you more than cheating:

🎲 Five strangers

One round each against five strangers you'll never meet again. After every round the meter shows what your other choice would have earned.

your coins 0
Stranger 1 of 5 is waiting.

So two perfectly “logical” players both cheat… and walk away with 1 coin each, staring at the 3-and-3 they could have had. That both-cheat outcome — where neither player can do better by changing only their own move — is called a Nash equilibrium, and the heartbreaking gap between it and both-share is the dilemma. It's not a party trick: countries stockpiling weapons, kids leaving the group project to the others, everyone littering “just one” wrapper — same game, same trap.

3 · The escape hatch: play it again

Everything above assumed one round against a stranger. But most of life isn't strangers — it's the same classmates, teammates and siblings, over and over. When the game repeats, today's cheat becomes tomorrow's reputation. Play 15 rounds against the same mystery partner and see if you can both get rich:

🤝 The long deal

Same partner, 15 rounds. Finish with 40+ coins for BOTH of you to earn the 🤝 Peacemaker badge. (Top row = you, bottom = partner.)

you 0partner 0
Round 1 of 15. Your move.
Spoiler (after you play): your partner was 🪞 Copycat — it starts friendly, then simply plays your last move back at you. Game theorists call it tit-for-tat. Notice what it does to the trap: against Copycat, cheating still grabs you 5 coins today — but it costs you 3 coins every round after, because your own cheat comes bouncing back. In a repeated game, the "dominant" move stops being dominant, and kindness-with-a-memory becomes the money-maker.

4 · Know your opponent

Different people play repeated games very differently. Meet the five classic personalities:

😇 Angel

Always shares. No matter what.

😈 Devil

Always cheats. No matter what.

🪞 Copycat

Shares first, then plays your last move back at you.

😡 Grudger

Shares — until you cheat once. Then cheats forever.

🪙 Coinflip

Flips a coin every round. Pure chaos.

🕵️ Guess who

Play 8 rounds against a mystery partner, then name them. Detective tip: everyone looks like an Angel while you're sharing — cheat once and watch what comes back. Streak of 3 correct = ⭐.

5 · The tournament — and the twist

In 1980, professor Robert Axelrod held exactly this contest: strategies from around the world, round-robin, most coins wins. Run ours — every personality plays every other for 10 rounds:

😈 wins — almost every run (if 🪙's lucky coin sneaks someone past him, run it again). Robbing Angels pays better than any friendship. So cheating is the best strategy after all…? Here's the twist that made this game famous. A tournament is one season — but life goes on. Suppose after each season the worst-scoring players quit, and the winners' strategy gets copied by newcomers (that's evolution — in playgrounds as much as jungles). Watch a town of 24 players, mostly trusting Angels with a few Devils and Copycats mixed in:

🧬 Evolution: 24 players, many seasons

Each season: everyone plays everyone (10 rounds), the 4 poorest players quit, and 4 newcomers copy the richest strategy.

Season 0 — a trusting town with a few bad apples. Press ▶.
The big idea: the Devils boom while there are Angels to rob — then run out of victims and starve on 1-coin handshakes with each other, and the town ends up all Copycats: everyone sharing, everyone rich, but nobody exploitable. Axelrod's real tournaments were won by exactly that strategy — tit-for-tat — and his famous recipe for winning repeated games reads like playground advice: be nice (never cheat first), hit back (don't be a sucker twice), forgive (one punch back, then let it go), and be clear (let people learn they can trust you). That's why repeated life is mostly cooperative even though every single day contains this trap.

📓 Homework

  1. Play it for real. Ten rounds against someone in your family, with real candy or coins and the payout table above (write moves secretly, reveal together). Then swap: you be Copycat and see if they figure you out.
  2. Spot the dilemma. Which of these are prisoner's dilemmas — where cheating is tempting but everyone-cheats is worst? Littering · doing your share of a group project · two shops both promising "lowest prices" · taking the last cookie.
  3. Break Copycat. Copycat has one weakness: if BOTH players are Copycats and one cheats once by accident, what happens for the rest of the game? (Play it out on paper.) What one-word fix repairs it? (Hint: it's in Axelrod's recipe.)

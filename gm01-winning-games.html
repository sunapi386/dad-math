Game Theory · Topic 01

How to Win Every Game (Sometimes)

Game theory is the math of out-thinking an opponent who is busy out-thinking you. It runs chess engines, auctions, penalty kicks, even how animals share territory. Today: a game you can learn to win every single time — and a game where the only unbeatable move is to stop thinking entirely.

⏱ ~30 min   no math track needed   🎲 beat a perfect robot

1 · The 21-stick game

Rules: 21 sticks. You and the robot take turns removing 1, 2 or 3 sticks. Whoever takes the last stick wins. The robot plays perfectly — but this game has a secret, and once you find it, the robot never stands a chance. Play a few rounds first and watch what numbers it always leaves you…

2 · The secret: think backwards

Don't start from 21 — start from the end and walk back:

If it's your turn and 1, 2 or 3 sticks remain — you just take them all. You win. Those are hot numbers.
If 4 remain — take 1, 2 or 3, and your opponent faces a hot number. You lose. 4 is cold.
If 5, 6 or 7 remain — take just enough to hand your opponent exactly 4. Hot again!
If 8 remain — anything you do hands them a hot number. Cold.

See it? The cold numbers are the multiples of 4:

That walk-from-the-end trick is called backward induction, and it's how computers crack games. The winning rule falls right out: always leave your opponent a multiple of 4 — take $n \bmod 4$ sticks (the remainder of $n \div 4$). From 21, the remainder is 1: take one stick, then copy — whatever the bot takes, take enough to total 4 together. It can never escape the cold ladder: 20, 16, 12, 8, 4… 0. Checkmate from move one.

Big idea: in games with no luck and no secrets (chess, checkers, tic-tac-toe, this one), somebody has a perfect strategy — the game is decided before it starts, if you can think backwards far enough. Checkers was fully cracked this way in 2007: perfect play by both sides is always a draw.

4 · When the only good plan is no plan

The stick game rewards pure logic. Rock-paper-scissors is the opposite: there is no clever move, because every move loses to something. Here the enemy isn't the rules — it's your own patterns. Humans are terrible at being random: we avoid repeats, we chase what just beat us. This bot exploits exactly that. It remembers what you tend to throw after each move… and plays the counter.

🤖 The pattern sniffer

Play at least 15 rounds. If the bot pulls ahead — it has learned your habits. The only strategy it can't beat is true randomness (game theory calls this a mixed strategy). Try playing with a mental coin-flip and watch it lose its edge.

Real-world: penalty kicks and tennis serves are studied with this exact math — pros really do randomize their aim, in almost exactly the proportions game theory predicts. Being predictable is the only way to lose at random games… and the only way to win at thinking games is the cold-number ladder. Knowing which kind of game you're in is game theory's first lesson.

📓 Homework

  1. Hustle a grown-up. Set up 21 real sticks (toothpicks, LEGO, spoons) and win three times in a row. Then — this is the hard part — teach them the cold numbers and let them beat you back.
  2. Change the rules, find the new colds. If you may take 1–4 sticks, which numbers are cold now? (Think backwards from 0. What's the pattern for take 1–k?)
  3. Spot the game type. Sort these into “thinking game — someone has a perfect strategy” vs “mixing game — you must be unpredictable”: tic-tac-toe · hide-and-seek · Connect 4 · poker bluffing · checkers.

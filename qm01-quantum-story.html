The Cat in the Box

A mostly-true story about the smallest things in the universe, told one chapter at a time. Read it aloud. Stop at each experiment and play.

📖 story mode   ⏱ ~30 min aloud   finish = 🐱 badge

Chapter 1 · The coin that couldn't decide

Once upon a time — which is how all the best physics begins — there was a girl named Nova who found a very old coin in her grandfather's desk. Taped to it was a note:

“This coin is not heads. It is not tails. Until you look, it is genuinely, stubbornly, BOTH. Looking is what makes it choose. —Grandpa”

Nova laughed. Everything is something, even when nobody's looking. The fridge light is off when the door is shut. Probably. She flipped the coin and slapped it onto the desk, hand covering it.

And she felt it — a tiny buzzing under her palm. Not a coin lying still, being secretly heads or secretly tails. A coin still humming with both possibilities at once.

Scientists have a word for that humming: superposition. The smallest things in the universe — electrons, photons, atoms — really do hum like this. Not "we don't know which" — truly neither and both, until a measurement forces the choice.

Chapter 2 · The two doors

In the desk drawer, under the coin, Nova found a strange toy: a little launcher, a wall with two doors, and a screen behind it that remembered every ball that hit it.

She fired thousands of tiny balls. Balls are sensible: each one goes through the left door or the right door, and you get two piles on the screen. And that's exactly what happened — as long as the little detector light on the doors was on, watching which door each ball used.

Then she switched the detector off. Nobody — nothing — watching.

The piles vanished. In their place: stripes. Many stripes, like ripples in a pond, including stripes in places no ball could sensibly reach. Each unwatched ball had gone through both doors at once — like the coin humming both answers — and its two possible paths had rippled into each other.

“The universe acts differently when you watch it,” said the note taped to the launcher. “This is not a metaphor. This is the actual result of the actual experiment.”

It's called the double-slit experiment, and it is the most famous experiment in all of physics. Try it yourself:

Chapter 3 · The gloves that decide together

The last thing in the drawer was a pair of small boxes and a letter.

“Nova — inside these boxes is a pair of quantum gloves. Like any pair, one is LEFT and one is RIGHT. Mail one box to your cousin in Australia. Then open yours.”

Nova did. When she opened her box and found a LEFT glove, she knew — instantly, faster than any message could travel — that the box in Australia held the RIGHT one. So far, not spooky: ordinary gloves work like that too. The pair was decided when it was packed.

But quantum gloves are not decided when they're packed. Each glove hums, LEFT-and-RIGHT at once, like Grandpa's coin — and careful experiments (this won a Nobel Prize in 2022) prove there is no secret answer hiding inside the box. Yet the moment one glove chooses, its twin — across the world, across the galaxy — always matches. They don't have answers; they share one.

Einstein hated this. He called it “spooky action at a distance” and spent years trying to prove it couldn't be real. The experiments say: it's real. It's called entanglement.

Chapter 4 · Why grandpa's desk matters

Nova sat back. A coin that hums both answers. Doors that behave differently when watched. Gloves that decide together across the world. Why would anyone build a desk full of this?

Because, said the last note, the whole universe is built of it. Every atom in your hand hums. The sun shines because particles do things that "shouldn't" be possible — they borrow the both-at-once trick to squeeze past barriers. The chip in your tablet only works because engineers learned the humming's rules.

And the newest machines — quantum computers — use humming coins (called qubits) instead of the ordinary bits from our binary lesson. A regular bit is 0 or 1. A qubit hums 0-and-1, and entangled qubits hum together — so a quantum computer can explore a huge maze of possibilities in one go, where a normal computer must walk the corridors one at a time.

“One warning,” the note ended. “Nobody — nobody — fully knows WHY looking makes the choosing happen. We know the rules perfectly; the reason is still a mystery. Maybe leave the mystery for the next scientist in this family. —Grandpa”

Nova closed the drawer, put the coin in her pocket, and felt it hum.

— The End (of chapter one of physics) —

Epilogue · What was true?

Nearly all of it. Superposition, the double-slit experiment, entanglement and the 2022 Nobel Prize, quantum tunnelling in the sun, qubits — all real, all tested thousands of times. The only made-up parts are Nova, the desk, and gloves big enough to see (real entangled things are particles). And the famous cat in the box of the title? That was Schrödinger's joke-experiment: put a cat's fate in the hands of one humming atom and the cat must hum too — alive-and-asleep at once — which he invented to show how weird the rules sound. The rules won anyway.

Words we learned

superposition · measurement · collapse (the choosing) · double-slit · entanglement · qubit · “spooky action at a distance”

Talk about it: if looking changes the answer… what does “looking” even mean? Does a camera count? A fly? There's no agreed answer — your guess is a real position some physicists hold.

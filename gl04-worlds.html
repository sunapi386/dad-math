Random Worlds

Deep Rock Galactic drops you into a fresh cave every single mission, and no two are ever alike — yet the game ships with zero caves inside it. It grows them on the spot from a pinch of randomness and a handful of rules. It's called procedural generation, and it's how Minecraft, Factorio, and No Man's Sky build worlds bigger than any hard drive.

⏱ ~30 min   the “how is it always different?” topic

1 · A drunk little digger

Here's the simplest cave-maker there is. Drop a digger in the middle of solid rock. Each step it picks a random direction — up, down, left, or right — and tunnels one square that way. Let it wander a few hundred steps and it leaves behind a twisty, cave-like hollow. Because it only ever steps to a touching square, everywhere it dug connects back — no walled-off pockets you can't reach.

🕳️ The cave grower

Press Dig a new cave a few times. Same rule every time — a totally different cave every time. That's the whole trick behind “infinite” worlds.

2 · The magic word: a seed

If it's random, how do two friends load the same Minecraft world? The secret is that game “randomness” isn't truly random — it's a recipe that starts from one number, the seed. Same seed in, exact same sequence of “random” steps out, so the exact same world. Change the seed and everything changes. Type a seed and dig — then type it again and get the identical cave.

🌱 Seeded caves

Enter any number as a seed. The same seed always grows the same cave — that's why seeds are shareable.

Pick a seed and grow it.
Try: grow seed 7, then hit Grow it again — pixel-for-pixel identical, because the recipe replays the same steps. Now change it to 8. A brand-new world. This is exactly how a Minecraft seed you find online gives you someone else's world.

3 · Randomness + rules = a world

Pure randomness looks like TV static — no fun. Real games tame it with rules: “caves must connect,” “don't spawn lava next to the start,” “put ore in clumps.” The art of procedural generation is picking a few good rules so every random world still feels fair and playable. Our one rule — the digger only steps to a touching square — already bought us a guarantee: the whole cave is reachable. Deep Rock stacks dozens of rules like that.

4 · 🧩 Puzzle: run the recipe

Game “dice” follow a fixed recipe. This one is: next = (5 × seed + 3) mod 16 — multiply, add, keep the remainder after dividing by 16. Run it. Streak of 3 = ⭐, streak of 5 = 🗺️ badge.

📓 Homework

  1. Hunt for a seed. Does a game you play let you type a seed (Minecraft does)? What changes when you change it? What stays the same?
  2. Be the digger. On graph paper, flip a coin for direction (heads = right/up, tails = left/down) and carve 30 squares. Did you make a cave? Is it all connected?
  3. Invent a rule. Our caves can look messy. Write one rule to improve them — e.g. “fill in any single leftover rock surrounded by floor.” That smoothing rule is real; it's called cellular automata.

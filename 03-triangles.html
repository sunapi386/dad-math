Lesson 03

Triangular Numbers

Ten bowling pins stack into a perfect triangle. So do 15, and 21, and 28… These “triangular numbers” hide inside handshakes, party games — and the exact same formula you derived in Lesson 1. Coincidence? Let's find out.

⏱ ~30 min   follows Lesson 1

1 · The bowling-pin question

Bowling uses 10 pins: a row of 1, then 2, then 3, then 4.

$$1 + 2 + 3 + 4 = 10$$

Numbers you can stack into a triangle like that are called triangular numbers. Question: if a giant bowling alley stacked 100 rows of pins, how many pins would it need? (Try to feel the Lesson 1 déjà vu…)

2 · Build them

🎳 Triangle builder

Slide to stack rows. Each new row (orange) adds its row-number of dots — row 5 adds 5 dots.

The totals go 1, 3, 6, 10, 15, 21, \dots — and the n-th one is just

$$T_n \;=\; 1 + 2 + 3 + \cdots + n \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{n} i$$
Wait a second. That's exactly the sum from Lesson 1! A triangular number isn't a new thing — it's the Gauss sum wearing a costume. So we already know the answer… but this time, let's see why the formula is true, with a picture.

3 · The picture proof: two triangles make a rectangle

Take the triangle (as a staircase of dots). Make a copy, flip it upside-down, and snap the two together. They fit perfectly into a rectangle that is n dots tall and n+1 dots wide.

🧲 Snap two triangles together

Purple = your staircase triangle. Orange = the flipped copy. Together: a perfect n × (n+1) rectangle — so one triangle is half of it.

Rectangle = n(n+1) dots. Triangle = half the rectangle:

$$\boxed{\;T_n = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}\;}$$

The same formula as Lesson 1 — but now you can see it. The giant bowling alley needs \tfrac{100 \times 101}{2} = 5050 pins. □

4 · The handshake problem

Here's where triangular numbers sneak into real life. If everyone at a party shakes hands with everyone else exactly once, how many handshakes happen?

Think about person-by-person: with p people, the 1st person shakes p-1 hands. The 2nd already shook the 1st, so they add p-2 new ones. Then p-3, and so on down to 1 and 0:

$$(p-1) + (p-2) + \cdots + 2 + 1 \;=\; T_{p-1} \;=\; \frac{(p-1)\,p}{2}$$
🤝 Handshake circle

Each dot is a person; each line is one handshake. Count the lines — always a triangular number.

5 · 🧩 Puzzle: pins & parties

Mixed round — sometimes pins, sometimes handshakes. Streak of 3 earns a ⭐, streak of 5 earns a badge.

📓 Homework

  1. List the first ten triangular numbers T_1 to T_{10} in your notebook. Check two of them with the formula.
  2. Your class: if everyone in a class of 20 high-fives everyone else once, how many high-fives? (Careful: is it T_{20} or T_{19}?)
  3. Challenge: add two neighbouring triangular numbers, like T_3 + T_4 = 6 + 10. Try a few pairs. What kind of number do you always get? (Hint: Lesson 2 knows.)

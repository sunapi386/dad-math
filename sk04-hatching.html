Shading with Lines, Wobbling on Purpose

A pencil can't pour gray paint — yet pencil drawings are full of grays. The trick is hatching: gray isn't a colour, it's a fraction. Tonight you'll shade with arithmetic, learn why the strokes must bend with the surface, and meet the strangest idea in the whole Sketch Lab: a machine that computes everything perfectly… and then adds mistakes on purpose.

⏱ ~30 min   fractions and percents   ✏️ mix gray from math

1 · Gray is a fraction

Zoom into pencil shading and it's just parallel strokes. If each stroke is w wide and they sit s apart, the fraction of paper covered in ink is

$$\text{tone} = \frac{w}{s} \qquad \text{(width 1, spacing 4} \Rightarrow \text{25\% gray)}$$

Want darker? Don't press harder — squeeze the spacing, or lay a second layer of strokes across the first (cross-hatching ≈ double the coverage), or a third (triple). The Studio engine offers exactly these: single / cross / triple, and it quantizes — instead of a smooth soup of grays it snaps every patch of a scene to a few honest tone levels, the way an artist commits: paper, light, medium, dark.

2 · Strokes have a grain

Here's what separates flat shading from drawing: on a curved thing, good hatching follows the surface. Artists shade a ball with strokes that bend like lines of latitude, and a pipe with strokes that wrap around it. Your eye reads the bending itself as 3D — the strokes don't just darken the shape, they describe it.

The engine does this by remembering, for every surface, its own natural grid — like the latitude/longitude lines on a globe or the rings and rays of a tree stump — and laying hatch strokes along it (field: true in the Studio). Switch to field: false and it rules flat parallel lines across everything instead — suddenly the ball looks like a printed circle, not a sphere. Same tone, worse story. Try exactly this in the Studio tomorrow.

Feel the grain: shade a toilet-paper tube with straight up-down strokes, then shade another wrapping the strokes around the curve. Hold both at arm's length — which one looks round? Show someone else, don't tell them which is which, and ask.

3 · Perfect wobble

Now the beautiful, backwards part. The engine computes outlines exactly (SK·2), removes hidden lines exactly (SK·3)… and then, if you ask, it wobbles every stroke so the drawing looks like a human held the pencil. Why ruin perfection? Because a ruler-straight drawing says “measured by a machine,” while a wobbly one says “seen by someone.” The imperfection is information about being drawn.

The person who built this engine (his name is Valeriu — you'll meet his work in the Studio) wanted to call it genesis imperfecta as a kid: the fascination isn't adding detail, it's choosing which detail to drop and which imperfections to add. That is an actual math question, and here's the engine's answer for wobble: the wiggle is seeded noise — the same trick as your Minecraft worlds! Same seed → the exact same wiggle, every single time. So an animation doesn't “boil” with new scribbles each frame, and the drawing is still, secretly, perfectly reproducible. Controlled chaos, chosen on purpose.

Try this tonight

  • The 25% experiment: rule strokes 4 mm apart with a ~1 mm pencil line. Step back — does it read as quarter-gray? Now cross-hatch a patch and compare.
  • Tone ladder: draw four squares: paper, single, cross, triple. You've built the engine's quantized tone scale with your own hand.
  • Forgery test: draw a freehand circle, then trace a coin. Show both to someone — which one do they say has more life? Ask them why. (There's no right answer. That's the point.)

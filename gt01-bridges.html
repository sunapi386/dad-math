The Seven Bridges

In the 1700s, the city of Königsberg had two islands and seven bridges. Sunday strollers played a game: cross every bridge exactly once. Nobody ever managed it. Then Leonhard Euler proved why nobody ever would — and accidentally invented a whole new kind of math.

2 · Euler's move: forget the city

Euler's genius was realizing the map doesn't matter. Shrink each landmass to a dot (a node) and each bridge to a line (an edge). The puzzle survives — everything else was decoration. A picture of dots and lines is called a graph, and it's the same object as Lesson 3's handshake circle.

Now count each node's degree — how many edge-ends touch it. Here's the key idea:

The walking rule: every time your stroll passes through a node, it uses up two of that node's edges — one in, one out. So a node with an odd degree can't be passed through cleanly forever: it must be where you start or where you end. And a walk only has one start and one end — so at most 2 odd nodes are allowed. Zero also works (a loop).

Königsberg's degrees: the island has 5, the other three landmasses have 3 each. That's four odd nodes. Four > two. Impossible — not "hard": impossible, proven forever, without trying a single route. □

3 · Now one that can be done

The classic “draw this without lifting your pen”: a house with an X inside. Count odd nodes first — the two bottom corners have degree 3. Exactly two odd nodes → possible, but you must start at an odd one.

4 · Where graphs live now

Every one of these is secretly the same dots-and-lines math: your GPS finding the fastest route · the internet routing this very page · friend networks (Lesson 3's handshakes!) · garbage-truck and mail routes (they want an Euler walk — no street twice) · molecules · subway maps. Euler's little stroll became the mathematics of every network on Earth.

📓 Homework

  1. Fix Königsberg: the city wants the Sunday stroll to work. Which ONE bridge would you add (or knock down!) to make it possible? Check the degrees after your change.
  2. Draw the envelope puzzle for a friend and watch them try from a top corner. Then reveal the rule.
  3. Challenge: your own house — doorways are bridges, rooms are nodes. Can you walk through every doorway exactly once? Count the degrees and predict before trying.

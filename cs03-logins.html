How Logins Keep Secrets

You just made a login to save your badges — you drew a secret shape. Here's the surprising part: the website never keeps your shape. It can't even see it. So how does it know it's really you next time? The answer is a beautiful one-way street called hashing — the exact opposite of Caesar's cipher from last time.

⏱ ~30 min   sequel to CS 2 · Secret Codes

1 · The problem: don't keep the secret

Imagine the website kept a notebook: “DragonKid's pattern is ↘↓↙”. Handy — until a thief steals the notebook and now knows everyone's secret. Real websites get their notebooks stolen all the time. So the trick is: check the secret without ever storing it. Sounds impossible, right?

2 · The one-way street: hashing

A hash is a math blender. Feed it anything — a word, a pattern, a whole book — and it spits out a fixed-size scramble of gibberish. Two rules make it magic:

  • Same in → same out. The blender is not random; the same secret always makes the same hash.
  • You can't un-blend. Given the gibberish, there's no way back to the secret. (You can't un-smoothie a smoothie.)

So the website stores only the hash of your pattern. Next time you draw it, it blends your drawing and checks: does this gibberish match the gibberish I saved? If yes — welcome back. The real secret was never written down anywhere.

🥤 The hash blender (real SHA-256)

Type anything and watch the blender. Then change one tiny thing and watch the whole hash explode into something totally different — that's the avalanche effect.

3 · Why not just use Caesar's cipher?

Last lesson you encrypted with a cipher — and then you decrypted it. That's the whole point of a cipher: it goes both ways. But that's exactly what we don't want here. If the website could unscramble your stored secret, so could a thief.

$$\text{cipher: } \texttt{CAT}\;\underset{\text{decrypt}}{\overset{\text{encrypt}}{\rightleftarrows}}\;\texttt{FDW} \qquad\qquad \text{hash: } \texttt{CAT}\;\xrightarrow{\ \text{blend}\ }\;\texttt{77af…9c2}\ \ \not\rightarrow$$

A cipher is a locked box (open it with the key). A hash is a paper shredder (no putting it back). For passwords, you want the shredder.

4 · Twins and salt

Here's a leak. If two kids both pick the pattern ↘↓↙, their hashes come out identical — and a thief who steals the notebook instantly spots the twins and knows they share a secret. Fix: before blending, the website sprinkles in a few random letters unique to each account, called a salt. Now the same pattern makes two totally different hashes.

🧂 Salt the twins

Two kids, the same secret pattern. Watch what salt does.

The honest bit: a drawn pattern only has a few thousand shapes — like Caesar's 25 keys, a computer could brute-force them (try them all) pretty fast. So this login is fun, not Fort Knox. That's exactly why it saves only your nickname and your badges — nothing precious — and why you should never reuse a real password (a bank one!) on a toy like this. Same lesson as CS 2: a secret is only as strong as how many guesses it takes.

5 · How your badges ride along

Once the website knows it's you, your stars and badges get to travel. When you log in on a new tablet, your device sends up its collection, the server merges it with what's saved — keeping the biggest star count and the union of all badges — and sends the whole thing back. So no device ever wipes another: play on the phone and the laptop, and everything lands in one pile. 🎒

📓 Homework

  1. Be the blender. Type your name into the hasher above, screenshot the gibberish. Now add one letter and hash again. How much of it changed — a little, or almost all?
  2. Why can't you un-hash? A blender turns fruit into smoothie. Explain in one sentence why you can't turn the smoothie back into fruit — and what that has to do with passwords.
  3. Count the shapes. On the 9-dot grid, a 4-dot pattern with no repeats has \(9\times 8\times 7\times 6\) choices. Work it out. Is that more or fewer than Caesar's 25 keys? Which is safer, and why?

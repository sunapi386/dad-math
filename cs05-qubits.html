Qubits: How Quantum Computers Count

In CS·1 you met the bit: a switch, 0 or 1, no in-between. Tonight, the upgrade the whole physics world is racing to build: the qubit — a bit that is an arrow, free to lean anywhere between 0 and 1 until you look. It's the Cat in the Box story, turned into a machine you can actually steer.

⏱ ~30 min   best after CS·1 + the cat story   🌀 hit par on real circuits

1 · A bit with a lean

A bit is a light switch: 0 or 1. A qubit is a dial. Point the arrow at the 0 mark — it's certainly 0. Point it at the 1 mark — certainly 1. Point it between them and the qubit is genuinely both-at-once (that's superposition, the spinning coin from the story). The lean is not a secret answer waiting to be revealed — the lean itself is the whole truth.

How much “1-ness” does a leaning arrow have? Here's the rule the universe picked, and it's a formula you've owned since Lesson 2 — a square:

$$\text{chance of seeing } 1 = (\text{how far the arrow leans toward } 1)^2$$

And because the arrow always has length 1, Pythagoras keeps the books balanced automatically: $a^2 + b^2 = 1$ means chance of 0 + chance of 1 = 100%, always. The universe does its accounting with the Pythagorean theorem.

Collapse is real: after a measurement the lean is gone — the arrow now points straight at whatever answer you saw, and measuring again just repeats it. You cannot peek at a qubit without changing it (remember the box in the story — opening it IS the event). One measurement gives you one bit, never the whole lean; the lean can only be estimated by preparing and measuring many times. Hold that thought for the detective game below.

2 · Steering the arrow: gates

A normal computer changes bits with logic gates (you built AND and XOR in Factory Logic). A quantum computer changes the lean with quantum gates. Two famous ones:

X — the flipper. Whatever the chances of 0 and 1 were, swap them. It's the quantum NOT: 0 becomes 1, 1 becomes 0, a 45° lean stays 45° (equal is equal, swapped or not).
H — the half-flipper. Take something certain and make it perfectly 50/50: it turns “definitely 0” into an exact coin-flip. This is how quantum computers create superposition on demand.

Now the trick that separates quantum randomness from every coin you've ever flipped. Flip a real coin twice — still random. But apply H twice to a qubit…

One H makes a perfect coin. What do TWO make?

H once: a true coin-flip. H twice: always 0. The randomness is undone. No real coin can do that — a coin flipped twice is just as random. Inside the qubit, the two “maybe” paths to 1 point in opposite directions along the circle and cancel out, while the paths to 0 agree and add up. That canceling is called interference, and it is the actual engine of every quantum computer: choreograph the maybes so wrong answers cancel and right answers pile up. (Bonus mystery in the third button: X then H is still 50/50 — but it leans the opposite way around the circle. Same chances, different arrow. That hidden minus sign is exactly what the second H can grab onto.)

3 · One look is never enough

Here's the strangest everyday fact of quantum work. A qubit's lean can hold any amount of 1-ness — but a measurement only ever answers 0 or 1. So how do scientists read a lean? They can't. 4 · Why the world wants these

Remember CS·1: $n$ bits can spell $2^n$ different numbers — but a normal computer's $n$ bits spell one of them at a time. Put $n$ qubits all in superposition and the machine holds a lean across all $2^n$ patterns at once. Ten qubits: 1,024 numbers. Fifty: a million billion. Then interference goes to work — cancel the wrong answers, amplify the right one — and one final measurement drops the result in your lap.

That's the honest version of the headlines: a quantum computer is not “trying everything and picking the best” — measurement would just give one random pattern. The art (and the reason it's hard) is choreographing the cancellation, like H,H writ large. Today's real machines have a few hundred noisy qubits and live in freezers colder than deep space — your tablet still beats them at everything you'd actually do. But for codes (remember CS·2's 77-digit numbers?), molecules and materials, the choreography is already written and waiting for the hardware. You now know the two moves it's built from: superposition and interference.

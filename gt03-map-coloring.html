The Map-Colouring War

In 1852 a student colouring a map of England noticed he never needed a fifth crayon — and asked why. That innocent question defeated the world's best mathematicians for 124 years, produced a famous wrong proof that stood for a decade, and was finally settled by a computer — the first great theorem in history proved by a machine. Mathematicians are still arguing about whether that counts.

⏱ ~30 min   best after G·1: Seven Bridges   🎨 paint maps at par

1 · One rule, one question

Map-makers follow one rule: countries that share a border must get different colours (touching at a single corner point doesn't count — only real shared borders). The question: what's the smallest box of crayons that can handle any map anyone could ever draw?

The 1852 claim, now the four-colour theorem: four crayons always suffice. Any map. Any number of countries. Any crazy shapes. Before reading why — earn it. Paint:

2 · Squash the map into dots

Here's the move that turns colouring into mathematics — the same move as G·1's bridges: throw away everything that doesn't matter. Shrink each country to a dot. Draw a line between two dots if their countries share a border. Shapes, sizes, wiggly coastlines — gone. The map has become a graph, and the puzzle becomes: colour the dots so no line connects two same-coloured dots.

Now we can actually reason. Why are four crayons sometimes needed? Because this map exists — three countries around one, every pair touching:

Four countries, every pair sharing a border — so all four need different colours. Four crayons are necessary. The war was over whether four are always enough. Notice what would settle it instantly: five countries, every pair touching. Try to draw that. Seriously — try it in the homework. It cannot be done on flat paper (or a globe): the fifth connection always has to cross another border. That impossibility is real progress… but it is not the proof. A map could conceivably need 5 crayons in some sneakier way, without containing five mutual touchers. Closing that gap took the full 124 years.

A theorem you can use at dinner: every flat map has some country with at most 5 neighbours (proved with Euler's formula from G·1 — everything connects!). That little fact powers the “peel one country off, colour the rest, put it back” strategy behind both the famous wrong proof and the real one.

3 · The war: 1852 → 1976

1879: Alfred Kempe publishes a proof. Applause; he's elected a Fellow of the Royal Society. 1890: Percy Heawood finds a hole in it — a case Kempe's colour-swapping trick silently gets wrong. Eleven years of “solved,” undone by one counterexample. (Kempe's broken proof still correctly shows five crayons always work — the gap between “5 is proven” and “4 is true” then sat open for almost a century.)

1976: Kenneth Appel and Wolfgang Haken finally close it — with a new weapon. They showed every possible map must contain at least one of 1,936 special local patterns, and that each pattern lets you shrink the map to a smaller one needing no fifth colour. Check all 1,936, and — since any smallest-possible counterexample would have to contain one — no counterexample can exist. One problem: checking them took 1,200 hours of computer time. No human has ever read the whole proof. No human ever will.

Mathematicians split on the spot, and the argument is still alive: if no person can check it, is it really knowing? Or is trusting a well-tested machine no different from trusting a well-tested telescope? Whatever side you take, 1976 marks the moment computers stopped being math's calculators and became its colleagues — today, machine-checked proofs guard everything from internet encryption to the code in aircraft.

4 · The lazy painter's guarantee

Proving “4 always works” is monstrous. But here's a guarantee you can prove tonight. Colour the dots one at a time, any order, always reusing the lowest-numbered crayon that doesn't clash — the greedy method (same spirit as the greedy chunk-growing in Minecraft Worlds). When you arrive at a dot with at most $d$ neighbours, at most $d$ crayons are forbidden — so crayon number $d+1$ is always free:

$$\text{if every region touches at most } d \text{ others, greedy never needs more than } d+1 \text{ crayons}$$

That's a real proof — three sentences, no computer. It gives a worse number (a map whose busiest region has 9 neighbours might get 10 crayons from greedy), but it's yours. The whole map-colouring war is the gap between an easy $d+1$ and a bitter, beautiful 4.

📓 Homework

  1. The impossible five. Try to draw 5 countries on paper where EVERY pair shares a border. Spend ten honest minutes. Then explain to someone where the fifth connection keeps getting trapped.
  2. Colour a real map. Print or trace a map of Canada's provinces (or the US states) and four-colour it. Which region forces the most re-thinking? (Hint: it's usually one with many neighbours — count them first, like a greedy painter.)
  3. Take a side. The 1976 proof: real proof, or just very strong evidence? Argue your side for one minute to a grown-up, then argue the OTHER side for one minute. (Mathematicians who can't do the second half lose the argument eventually.)

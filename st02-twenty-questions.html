Twenty Questions vs. the Universe

A story about the strangest discovery of the twentieth century: that every secret in existence — numbers, songs, photographs, you — is just a game of twenty questions. If you ask perfectly.

📖 story mode   ⏱ ~30 min aloud   finish = ❓ badge

Chapter 1 · The bet

Milo's uncle Max claimed, every single holiday, to be a mind reader. This year Milo came prepared.

“Fine,” said Milo. “I'm thinking of a number. Not up to a hundred — up to a MILLION. Read my mind.”

“Twenty questions,” said Uncle Max, cracking his knuckles. “Yes-or-no answers. If I don't name your number exactly, I do your chores for a month.”

A million numbers. Twenty questions. Milo did some quick multiplication and accepted instantly. Numbers were his thing. A million divided by twenty is fifty thousand — even if every question somehow eliminated a full fifty thousand numbers, Max would still be drowning. The chores were as good as done.

Uncle Max asked his first question, and it was a strange one.

“Is your number less than or equal to five hundred thousand?”

Chapter 2 · The halving avalanche

Max got it. Of course he got it. Milo demanded a rematch and Max got it again — eighteen questions that time. Milo stormed off to his room with a pencil, and here is what he found.

His mistake was thinking a question subtracts. “Is it 39?” subtracts — one number gone, 999,999 to go. But Max's questions never asked about a number. They asked about half the numbers at once. Answer YES or NO and either way, half the possibilities die. A perfect question doesn't subtract. It divides.

And dividing is an avalanche in reverse. You've met this monster from the other side — the doubling in CS·1, the reach explosion in Six Handshakes. Halving is how you kill a million quickly:

1,000,000 → 500,000 → 250,000 → 125,000 → …

How many halvings until one suspect remains? That's asking: how many doublings build a million? $2^{20} = 1{,}048{,}576$. Twenty. Uncle Max wasn't reading minds. He was halving them — with one question to spare.

Chapter 3 · The detective school

Milo came back downstairs with a plan: master the perfect question, then never lose again. Uncle Max, delighted, pulled out a deck of sixteen monster cards.

“The rules of detective school. I pick a secret monster. Every question you ask costs a point. Amateurs ask ‘Is it Zork?’ — that splits the deck fifteen to one, and fifteen-to-one questions are almost always wasted. Professionals ask about features, and count the split before they ask.”

“Sixteen monsters,” said Milo slowly, “is two-to-the-fourth… so a perfect game is four questions. Every time. No luck needed.”

“Prove it,” said Uncle Max.

Chapter 4 · The man who weighed questions

What Milo worked out at the kitchen table, a quiet engineer named Claude Shannon worked out for the whole universe in 1948 — in a paper many call the founding document of the information age.

Shannon's move was to give the answer of one perfect yes/no question a name: one bit. The same bit from our binary lesson — because a bit IS a recorded answer to a yes/no question. And then the wild part: he showed everything can be measured in bits. A number up to a million is 20 bits, whoever you are. A photo is a few million yes/no answers about pixels. A song, a video call, a spacecraft photo of Jupiter — all of it is stored, sent, and protected as answers to enormous games of twenty questions.

Shannon went further, into the idea Milo brushed against with his fifteen-to-one split: questions about predictable things are cheap. If a monster deck had twelve spotted monsters and four plain, “spots?” splits it badly — but you can design better questions for that deck and finish in fewer than four on average. That's why a predictable photo (all blue sky) squeezes into fewer bits than static noise. Every ZIP file, every streamed video, every “compressing…” bar you have ever watched is someone asking a lopsided deck better questions.

“One more rematch?” said Uncle Max, shuffling the monsters.

“No,” said Milo, taking out his own deck. “I pick the monster. YOU count the splits.” And from the way his uncle grinned, Milo knew he'd finally asked the right question.

— The End (answer: YES) —

Epilogue · What was true?

All of the math. $2^{20} = 1{,}048{,}576$, so twenty perfect questions really do corner any number up to a million — try the machine on a grown-up. Claude Shannon (1916–2001) is real, his 1948 paper A Mathematical Theory of Communication is real, he named the bit (crediting his colleague John Tukey for the word), and information theory now runs your phone, the internet, and every photo that survives a trip from Mars. Shannon also built a mind-reading machine, juggled while riding a unicycle through Bell Labs, and wired a mechanical mouse that learned mazes — all true, and all excellent homework gossip.

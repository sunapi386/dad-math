Why the Angry Bird Flies in an Arc

Pull the slingshot, let go, and the bird swoops through the air in a graceful curve to smash the pig's tower. It never flies in a straight line — always that same arch. Why? Because two things happen at once, and adding them makes the most famous curve in physics: the parabola.

⏱ ~30 min   builds on 🎮 GL·1 velocity & gravity

1 · Two motions at the same time

Once the bird leaves the slingshot, nothing pushes it sideways anymore. So its sideways speed just stays the same — steady, forever (until it hits something). But gravity never stops tugging it down, and that downward pull gets stronger every single moment.

  • ➡️ Sideways: constant speed — equal steps, tick, tick, tick.
  • ⬇️ Down: gravity speeds it up — the drops get bigger and bigger.

Glue those two together and the path bends: it rises, slows, tips over the top, then dives — faster and faster — into the pig. That shape is a parabola, the same curve you met with the square numbers (1, 4, 9, 16… — gravity uses those very jumps!).

2 · Fire the slingshot

🐦 The slingshot

Set the angle and power, watch the dotted aim line, then Launch and try to splat the pig 🐷. The bird traces its arc as it flies.

Aim with the dotted line, then launch!
Try: keep the power the same and slide the angle up from low to high. The landing spot goes out, reaches its farthest at 45°, then comes back in again — so a low 30° shot and a steep 60° shot land in the same place! One skims low and fast, the other loops high and slow.

3 · The math behind the arc

Call the launch speed \(v\) and the angle \(\theta\). Split the speed into its two jobs — going out and going up:

$$x = \underbrace{v\cos\theta}_{\text{sideways speed}}\; t \qquad\qquad y = \underbrace{v\sin\theta}_{\text{upward speed}}\; t \;-\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,g\,t^2$$

The sideways part is a plain steady march (\(x\) grows evenly with time \(t\)). The upward part climbs, but that \(-\tfrac{1}{2}gt^2\) — gravity — keeps eating it, more and more, until the bird comes back down. Together they draw the parabola. Do a little algebra and the range (how far it lands) works out to:

$$\text{range} = \frac{v^2 \sin(2\theta)}{g}$$

That \(\sin(2\theta)\) is the whole secret. It's biggest when \(2\theta = 90^\circ\), i.e. \(\theta = 45^\circ\) — the farthest shot. And since \(\sin\) is a mirror around there, \(30^\circ\) and \(60^\circ\) (which add up to \(90^\circ\)) give the exact same range. The math predicts what the slingshot just showed you.

📓 Homework

  1. Prove it in the game. In Angry Birds (or any throwing game), launch the same bird low-and-flat, then high-and-loopy, so both land on the same target. Which one gets there first? Why?
  2. Be the two motions. Drop a coin off the table and, at the very same instant, flick a second coin sideways off the edge. Listen — do they hit the floor together or one first? What does that tell you about the sideways motion and the falling?
  3. Think ahead: real Angry Birds birds slow down a bit from air resistance, and some (the yellow one!) speed up when you tap. How would each change the neat 45° rule?

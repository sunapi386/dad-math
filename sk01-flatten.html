Flattening Space

This is the first lesson of the Sketch Lab: over four nights you'll learn how a machine can draw like an artist — outlines, hidden lines, pencil shading — and at the end you get a real sketching engine to play with. But every drawing starts with one brutal problem: the world is 3D and paper is flat. Tonight: the single division that solves it.

⏱ ~30 min   good after GL·5 (angles) — not required   📐 spin a wireframe cube

1 · The window trick

Close one eye and hold a finger at arm's length: it can cover a whole car across the street. Renaissance painters turned this into a machine: look at the world through a window, and paint each thing exactly where its line of sight crosses the glass. A photo, a video game frame, and a pencil sketch all do the same thing — the window is the picture plane.

The math of the window is one division. Walk a thing of height S to distance d, and on the glass it measures

$$\text{size on the glass} = \frac{f \cdot S}{d}$$

where f is just how far the glass is from your eye — a fixed zoom number. All the perspective in every painting ever made is hiding in that divide by d: twice as far → half as tall. Ten times as far → a tenth as tall.

🤖 The shrink ray (that isn't one)

At 2 m the robot fills the window: 160 ÷ 2 = 80 px.
Say it like a mathematician: the robot never shrinks — the angle it eats out of your view shrinks. Similar triangles: the eye–robot triangle and the eye–glass triangle have the same shape, so glass-size / f = real-size / d. Cross-multiply and you've re-invented the formula.

2 · Two kinds of flattening

There's a choice hiding in the window trick. Perspective divides by each point's own distance — near parts get big, far parts get small, exactly like your eye. Orthographic refuses to divide at all — it slides every point straight onto the glass, so far things stay just as big as near things. Engineers love orthographic (you can measure the drawing: a ruler on the paper is a ruler on the machine); artists mostly want perspective (it's how seeing feels). A sketching machine has to offer both.

Spot it in the wild: railway tracks meeting at the horizon = perspective (divide by bigger and bigger d). Minecraft's inventory item icons and IKEA instructions = orthographic (measurable, no vanishing point). Which one is your favourite game's map screen?

Try this tonight

  • Thumb measuring: artists hold up a pencil at arm's length to measure the world — that's reading sizes off the picture plane. Measure a lamppost in “pencil units,” walk to half the distance, measure again. Did it double?
  • Window painter: with a washable marker, actually trace a building on a real window, one eye closed, head very still. You are doing exactly what the render engine does.
  • Find the vanishing point in a photo of a straight street: extend the road edges with a ruler — they cross at one point. Orthographic drawings have none. Why?

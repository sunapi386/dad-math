The Pirate Split

Five pirates. One hundred gold coins. A crew so logical it's terrifying. Read it aloud, and at every vote machine, stop and try to beat the captain.

📖 story mode   ⏱ ~30 min aloud   finish = 🏴‍☠️ badge

Chapter 1 · The Code

The Salty Theorem dropped anchor at midnight with a hundred gold coins and five pirates who did not trust each other even slightly.

In order of rank: 🦜 Captain Greybeard, first mate 🐙 Silver, then 🦈 Bones, then 🪝 Patch, and finally 🐀 Pegleg, who once got demoted for asking a thoughtful question.

Pirate law is very clear about treasure. The Code:

One — the highest-ranked pirate proposes a split.
Two — ALL pirates vote on it, proposer included. Half or more in favour: it passes, gold is paid, done.
Three — if the vote fails… the proposer walks the plank, and the next-ranked pirate proposes.

And you must know three things about these five. They are perfectly greedy — more gold is always better. They are perfectly logical — each one can calculate exactly what every other pirate will do, forever. And they are bloodthirsty — if the gold comes out the same either way, a pirate will always vote to watch someone walk the plank. For fun.

Captain Greybeard looked at the chest, looked at his crew, and smiled a slow, gold-toothed smile. He had already worked out the answer.

Out of 100 coins, how many does Captain Greybeard end up keeping? Lock in your prediction — the story will judge you at the end.

Chapter 2 · Think from the end

Here is the captain's secret, and it's the same trick that beat the 21-stick game in How to Win Every Game: when a game runs in rounds, don't think forwards. Think from the END backwards.

Imagine the worst has happened: three splashes. Only 🪝 Patch and 🐀 Pegleg remain, and Patch proposes. Two voters, so one vote is “half or more” — and Patch votes for himself.

“I propose,” said Patch, in this imaginary future, “that I get all one hundred coins, and Pegleg gets a warm feeling of participation.”

Pegleg votes NO with every fibre of his body. It changes nothing. One vote out of two is a tie, ties pass, Patch buys a solid-gold parrot.

Try to save Pegleg — be Patch, and see if generosity is ever forced:

⚓ Vote machine · 2 pirates

Split 100 coins between Patch and Pegleg, then call the vote. Can Pegleg's NO ever sink the plan?

Machine ready. Rank rules the seas.
Chapter 3 · One coin buys a rat

Now step back one splash. Three pirates: 🦈 Bones proposing, Patch and Pegleg voting. Three voters — Bones needs two votes. His own, plus one more. Which one is for sale, and at what price?

Every pirate on that deck has read Chapter 2. Patch is thinking: vote this down, Bones swims, and I get ALL 100. Patch's vote would cost Bones everything. But Pegleg is thinking something much sadder: vote this down and next round I get… zero.

“Pegleg,” said Bones sweetly, “I'm offering you one whole coin. That's one more than Patch ever will.”

One coin. More than zero. Greed says AYE — and remember, an equal offer wouldn't work: zero coins now versus zero coins later, and bloodthirst votes for the splash. Bones needs to beat Pegleg's future by exactly one coin, and no more, because greed cuts both ways. Find Bones's cheapest winning plan:

⚓ Vote machine · 3 pirates

Be Bones. Find a plan where he keeps 99 and survives. Then try bribing Patch instead of Pegleg and watch it sink.

Two votes needed. One is free. Shop wisely.
Chapter 4 · The pattern locks in

Step back again. Four pirates: 🐙 Silver proposes; Bones, Patch and Pegleg vote. Four voters — Silver needs two votes total, so just one ally. Everyone has read Chapter 3. If Silver sinks, the future is Bones's plan: Bones 99, Patch 0, Pegleg 1.

So who's cheap? Bones expects 99 — unbuyable. Pegleg expects 1, so he costs 2. But Patch expects 0. Patch — who in Chapter 2 dreamed of the whole chest — has become the cheapest pirate on the boat. One coin, and he's Silver's best friend.

“It's not personal,” said Silver, sliding one coin across the deck. “It's arithmetic.”

Notice the eerie flip-flop: the pirates who get a coin in one round expect a coin in the next round — which makes them expensive, so the proposer skips them and buys the ones expecting nothing. The gold zigzags down the ranks.

⚓ Vote machine · 4 pirates

Be Silver. Keep 99 and live. Then try being generous to Pegleg instead and see why it wastes a coin.

One ally needed. The cheapest pirate expects nothing.
Chapter 5 · The captain's smile

Back to the real deck, all five pirates, the chest unopened. Captain 🦜 Greybeard needs three of five votes: his own plus two allies. And he can see the whole chain of imaginary futures you just walked through. If he sinks, Silver's plan rules: Silver 99, Bones 0, Patch 1, Pegleg 0.

Who expects nothing in that future? Bones and Pegleg. One coin each. Done.

“Gentlemen,” said the captain, “my proposal. Ninety-eight for me. Nothing for Silver — he's rich in dreams. One for Bones. Nothing for Patch. One for Pegleg. Do I hear an AYE?”

Prove him right — or find something better. The machine is honest; if any plan lets the captain keep more and live, it will pass it:

⚓ Vote machine · all 5 pirates

Be Greybeard. Survive with 98 — ⭐. Then try to beat it with 99 (the machine will enjoy the splash). Every coin of bribe must beat someone's imaginary future by exactly one.

Three votes to find. Two pirates expect nothing. You know what to do.
Epilogue · What was true?

The puzzle is a classic of game theory and the answer really is 98 · 0 · 1 · 0 · 1 — the pirate everyone expects to lose everything walks away with almost everything, because he moves first and thinks last-to-first. The method is called backward induction: solve the final round, then the one before, all the way to the start. It's how chess engines value positions, how economists predict auctions and negotiations, and how you already beat the 21-stick robot.

But run this with real humans and something wonderful breaks. In laboratory versions (economists call the two-player one the ultimatum game), people offered one insulting coin out of a hundred usually say “NO — sink him” — happily paying a coin to punish greed. Real people carry fairness, anger and reputation into the room, and a wise captain knows it: offers below about a third get people voted overboard in every laboratory on Earth.

So the theorem is true about perfectly logical pirates, and the experiment is true about us — and knowing both is what makes you dangerous at the negotiating table.

— The End (mind the plank on your way out) —
